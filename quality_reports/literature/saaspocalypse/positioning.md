# Positioning Statement — "Another Model, Another SaaSpocalypse"
# MSc Finance Dissertation — Trinity College Dublin
# Last updated: 2026-05-19

---

## Suggested Contribution Statement

This dissertation makes four contributions to the literature on AI model releases and financial markets. First, it provides the first equity event study that explicitly contrasts SaaS firms against matched non-SaaS software firms around a single, precisely dated large-language-model release — the Anthropic Claude Opus 4.6 launch of February 5, 2026 — documenting the sign, magnitude, and cross-sectional heterogeneity of cumulative abnormal returns using a Fama-French five-factor framework with Kolari-Pynnönen cross-correlation corrections. Second, it tests the three-phase price dynamics predicted by Pástor and Veronesi (2009) against the actual event-time return history of SaaS firms, providing the first direct application of the technological-revolutions framework to the generative AI shock and its corporate software incumbents. Third, it implements a matched-sample difference-in-differences design using the Goldsmith-Pinkham and Lyu (2025) synthetic control framework, treating SaaS membership as the treatment and non-SaaS software firms as the comparison group, and validates the parallel-trends assumption with pre-event dynamic plots following Roth (2022); this causal design addresses the factor-model misspecification concern that standard event studies cannot resolve. Fourth, it extends the analysis to the corporate bond market using the Bessembinder-Kahle-Maxwell-Xu (2009) matched-portfolio framework with Ederington-Guan-Yang (2015) standardization, examining whether SaaS credit spreads widened in response to the AI release, thereby testing whether the equity disruption was also priced in the credit market — a question left open by Andrews and Farboodi's (2025) aggregate Treasury-yield event study.

## Differentiation from Closest Papers

The dissertation is differentiated from Conlon, Corbet and Muñiz (2025) by its SaaS-specific treatment definition, its causal DiD design, and its bond pillar. It is differentiated from Eisfeldt, Schubert and Zhang (2023/2025) by focusing on the disruption channel — how AI releases hurt software incumbents — rather than the adoption channel where AI-exposed firms gain. It is differentiated from Andrews and Farboodi (2025) by using individual SaaS issuer credit spreads rather than aggregate Treasury yields, and by combining the bond results with an equity event study for the same firm sample. It is differentiated from Bertomeu et al. (2023/2025) by studying an AI release rather than a regulatory ban, by focusing on a US equity context, and by adding the Pástor-Veronesi phase decomposition. No existing paper combines all four elements: SaaS-specific treatment contrast, three-phase Pástor-Veronesi architecture, Goldsmith-Pinkham-Lyu causal DiD design, and BKMX/EGY bond event study.
