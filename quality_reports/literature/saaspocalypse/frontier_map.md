## Where the Literature Stands

The empirical literature on AI model releases and equity markets is nascent but moving quickly. As of early 2026, four strands have converged near this dissertation's research question without fully overlapping it.

**Strand A — AI release event studies (general).** Conlon, Corbet & Muñiz (2025, SSRN) and Bertomeu et al. (2023/2025, JAE) establish that GenAI releases generate abnormal equity returns beyond standard factor-model expectations. Eisfeldt, Schubert & Zhang (2023/2025, JF forthcoming) provide the most credible causal estimate using a workforce-exposure measure (AMH portfolio): a 5% return in the two weeks after ChatGPT's release. Pietrzak (2025) and Wu et al. (2025, FRL) extend this to ChatGPT disclosures in SEC filings and to the DeepSeek R1 shock, respectively. Andrews & Farboodi (2025, NBER 34243) pivot to the bond market, documenting significant long-maturity yield declines around 15 AI releases. **What none of these studies does:** isolate SaaS from non-SaaS software, test within-SaaS heterogeneity, apply a matched DiD design treating SaaS firms as the treated cohort, or jointly study equity and credit markets for the same event.

**Strand B — Theoretical anchor (Pástor & Veronesi).** Pástor & Veronesi (2009, AER), building on their 2003 (JF) and 2006 (JFE) papers, provide the general equilibrium model in which technology revolutions generate a three-phase price pattern: (1) initial discount-rate shock as systematic uncertainty rises, (2) deepening as adoption spreads, (3) reversal as cash-flow learning resolves uncertainty. The empirical literature has applied this framework to the railroad and internet revolutions but not yet to the GenAI revolution. Hsu, Wang & Yang (2022, JMCB) document GPTs as a systematic risk factor, consistent with Phase 1 dynamics. **Gap:** no paper tests the full three-phase Pástor-Veronesi architecture against the AI revolution's actual price history using event-study methodology with clean phase boundaries.

**Strand C — Event study methodology.** MacKinlay (1997), Boehmer, Musumeci & Poulsen (1991), Kothari & Warner (2007), and Corrado (1989) establish the canonical tools. Kolari & Pynnönen (2010, RFS) correct for cross-sectional correlation — critical when all SaaS firms react to the same event simultaneously. Goldsmith-Pinkham & Lyu (2025, arXiv) demonstrate that factor-model misspecification during volatile periods biases abnormal return estimates, motivating synthetic control and DiD alternatives. The DiD methods revolution (Callaway & Sant'Anna 2021; Goodman-Bacon 2021; Sun & Abraham 2021; Roth et al. 2023) provides the robust causal tools for Pillar 3. **Gap:** no applied finance paper in the AI event literature implements a fully rigorous matched-sample DiD with pre-trend validation for a single, precisely timed AI release event.

**Strand D — Corporate bond event study.** Bessembinder, Kahle, Maxwell & Xu (2009, RFS) and Ederington, Guan & Yang (2015, JBF) provide the matched-portfolio bond event study methodology. Jorion & Zhang (2007, 2009) establish the credit-contagion framework for interpreting intra-industry bond reactions. Campbell & Taksler (2003, JF) provide the equity-volatility channel for bond yield movements. Mueller, Riehl, Buschulte & Weiss (2024, SSRN) address small-N bond event study power, which is the binding constraint for a SaaS bond pillar. **Gap:** no paper applies bond event study methodology to an AI model release event, and no paper simultaneously studies equity and bond market reactions for the same event-firm sample.

## Where This Dissertation Fits

The dissertation sits at the intersection of all four strands and fills four distinct gaps simultaneously: (1) identifying SaaS vs. non-SaaS software as the relevant treatment-control contrast; (2) testing the Pástor-Veronesi three-phase architecture against a precisely dated AI release; (3) applying Goldsmith-Pinkham & Lyu (2025) DiD methodology to an AI event study; and (4) extending the analysis to corporate bond markets using the BKMX/EGY framework. No existing paper occupies this intersection.

The principal scooping risk comes from Conlon et al. (2025) and Eisfeldt et al. (2025), which are closest in spirit but lack the SaaS-specific focus, the DiD causal design, and the bond pillar. The Andrews & Farboodi (2025) bond event study uses aggregate Treasury yields rather than individual SaaS issuer credit spreads, so the credit-channel question remains open. The dissertation's differentiation is therefore cumulative: four pillars that individually match existing papers in design quality, but whose combination — SaaS-specific, three-phase, causal DiD, and bond markets — has no prior work.
